The Perfection Paradox

On variation, existence, and the universe's only design

Kobe Anderson · 2026

My research advisor has a habit of letting conversations drift past the experiment. We would start with the data, a rat returning to the same quadrant of the maze three weeks after the platform was removed, a statistical outlier that refused to behave, a result that complicated everything we thought we understood. Somewhere in the back-and-forth we would end up somewhere else entirely. Not off topic, but deeper into it. The kind of conversation where the boundary between the science and the world dissolves and you stop being able to tell which of the two you are actually talking about.

He pushed me, consistently and without apology, to stop treating variance as error. To stop reaching for the clean result, the tidy effect, the data that confirms what you hoped to find. Variance is information, he would say. It is not the thing standing between you and the answer. It is the answer. I heard this instruction as methodology for a long time before I understood it was something larger. Before I understood that it applied not just to the rats in the maze but to everything I was struggling to make sense of like the state of the world, the persistence of suffering, the gap between what humanity is and what it keeps insisting it should be. The instruction was the same. Stop treating variance as the problem. It is not the problem. It is the point.

I spend my days studying why brains may possibly break. Not breaking in a literal sense but break in the way that traps a person in a loop they cannot exit, checking the same lock, running the same thought, returning to the same coordinates in a water maze long after the platform has been removed. In our lab, rats do what humans do: they persist past the point of reason. We call this compulsive behavior. We map it to corticostriatal circuits, to the balance between excitation and inhibition, to the dorsomedial and dorsolateral striatum pulling in opposite directions. We study it because we believe, with some justification, that understanding it will help us fix it.

But I have started to wonder whether fixing it is the right motive. Whether the impulse to resolve, to simplify, to perfect (within the brain, in the world, in the universe) is itself the thing worth examining.

We are the variation necessary for what we would call a perfect universe. I have believed this for some time without knowing how to convey it.

The statement sounds comforting. It really isn't. It is an unsettling and rather sobering hypotheisis on the nature of existence that I arrived at not through data but through the pressure built from lived experience. The kind of pressure that builds up when you think too abstractly for too long and start to notice that everything is connected in ways that we cannot and maybe are not supposed to fullycomprehend. The same kind of thinking that some would label as socially deviant or maybe even deranged.

Consider what a universe without variation would be. Not dark. Not silent. Not cold. Just perfectly uniform. In other words, indistinguishable, from nothing at all. No differentiation means no time, no structure, no observation, no experience. The absence of variation is not a peaceful universe. It is the absence of universe entirely. So then, variation is not a product of chaos or flaws. It is a necessity of evolution. Fire does not desire heat. Heat is a property of fire. Variation is a property of existence.

In behavioral neuroscience, sex as a biological variable (the NIH SABV mandate) exists precisely because variation between males and females produces meaningfully different outcomes. We do not treat this variation as noise to eliminate. We treat it as signal to understand. The science reflects the framework that variation is not error. It is information.

Medicine tells the same story. Every treatment ever studied shows individual variation in response. Antidepressants work for roughly half the people who try them. The same dose of the same drug produces different outcomes in different bodies. Identical twins with identical diagnoses respond differently to identical treatments. The entire direction of modern medicine is moving away from universal cures toward treatments matched to individual variation (precision medicine, personalized psychiatry, theragnostics). Not because science failed to find the universal answer. Because biology refuses to give one. This is not medicine's unsolved problem. It is medicine finally understanding the terms of the problem.

This reframes everything we call suffering. Conflict, contradiction, inequality, the gap between what is and what should be are not flaws in the system. They are the system. A mind shaped by evolution to avoid pain will frame pain as wrong, as correctable, as evidence of imperfection. But the universe did not design pain as a mistake. It designed it as difference. And difference is the only thing standing between existence and nothing.

The universe is not moving toward perfection. It is already perfect and it is perfect precisely because it is incomplete. Completion would be its end.

The pursuit of perfection is, in its essence, a slow erasure.

This is what I think about when I study behavior related to OCD. The compulsion is not irrational. It is the mind doing what humanity does, trying to resolve uncertainty, to achieve a final state, to close the loop. The tragedy is not the behavior. It is the destination it reaches for. A completeness that, if achieved, would extinguish the very consciousness seeking it. The rats in our lab circle the maze long after the platform is gone not because they are broken but because they are doing what minds do: searching for the thing that will make the searching stop. There is no such thing. That is not a failure. That is the design.

We are not passive observers of a universe that exists independently of us. We are the universe observing itself. Consciousness may be variation becoming aware of its own presence, the universe exploring its possibility space through beings capable of asking why. The search for purpose is not separate from the universe. It is the universe doing what it does: varying, differentiating, becoming something rather than nothing.

I have no data for this. I want to be clear about that. This is a stance, a lived experience that makes arguments feel beside the point. What I know from the bench is that variation is the engine of everything we study. What I know from existing is that the questions I cannot answer are the ones that have never stopped moving in me. And what I believe, without being able to prove it, is that this is not a problem to solve.

It is proof of life.
